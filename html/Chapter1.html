Diary and Cultural
Encounter.

By mammeta
14/06/2mai

Before moving to the United Kingdom to pursue my university degree my relationship with the English language has always been limited to what I did in school and the content and information I consumed on the internet. Because of that the English that I learned to speak, read and understand has always been a mixture of different variants, some related to the cultures that speak English natively and some from cultures that, like me, had to learn it as a second language.

The way I interacted and used the language on my day to day life had shaped the words and expressions that I learned and the meaning that I gave to them. Another important factor in the way I shaped the meaning of the expressions that I learned were the groups that I interacted with on the internet and therefore the people that shared my interests.

My background and my inclination for STEM disciplines also shaped my view of the world in a way that does not give too much importance to humans and how they relate to each other thru mediums like language. My business related studies reinforced this perception by limiting the role of people as simple actors in an economic model or a company setting.

The obvious limitations of the method in which I learned English caused a lack of understanding of certain cultural aspects of the United Kingdom related to expressions specific to British English and the underlying meaning of them. My confusion was most visible in regard to phatic communication, with some expressions still causing me problems today I decided to explore this topic to better analyse the functions of phatic communication, the interconnection between culture and phatic expressions and how these expressions evolved in the age of the internet.

The phatic expression that I found most challenging and curious in British English is "You alright?" used by everyone has a greeting to break the silence, one of the 12 functions of phatic communication that are (1) breaking the silence, (2) starting a conversation, (3) making small talk, (4) making gossip, (5) keeping talking, (6) expressing solidarity, (7) creating harmony, (8) creating comfort, (9) expressing empathy, (10) expressing friendship, (11) expressing respect, (12) expressing politeness (Jumanto 2014). The discrepancy between the phrase itself and the underlying meaning of it is pretty obvious when you are a native speaker, but but before I learned what it actually meant I always wondered why everyone was wondering if I was alright and never interpreted it as just a greeting until I asked one of my housemates why everyone always uses this expression when meeting me.

After my housemate explained that to me my perception of the English language and how certain expressions relate to the culture of native speakers widened and I realised that there was much more for me to learn of the English language to properly understand the people around me in this new environment and that the learning would never stop since the more cultures I explored the more I had to learn.
